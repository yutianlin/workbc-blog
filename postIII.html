Representation of Women in Senior Management

  • Part III
April 8, 2020

Why is it so difficult to have equal representation of men and women in senior management?


After Sarah has been engaged through her education process and has been hired by her dream company, she must overcome many more hurdles in her career. Like most women, she wishes to be recognized, either by promotion into management or by increased compensation while having a work-life balance.


Women's Interest and Barriers to Career Advancement

These statistics shows that women are interested and involved in their careers. It also shows how many of them are overlooked especially when they become new mothers.


Studies have shown that employee engagement is critical in an organization and is the driver behind innovation and operational success. It is a wonder as to why senior women are disproportionately negatively influenced by disengagement. In other words, when an organization is more engaged as a whole, women in senior positions are more engaged then men in senior positions, whereas the engagement are about equal when the organization is less engaged.


There are seven areas which shows this relationship, evaluated through a question each asked to both men and women in nonmanager, manager and senior manager positions. Each has a higher engagement score for women in nonmanager role, similar for manager, and higher from men in senior manager role.


Area Question
Appreciation “At work, my opinions seem to count.”
Work-Life Balance “The people I work with are considerate of my life outside of work.”
Cooperation and Good Relations with Colleagues “My manager is honest in dealing with others.”
Mentorship, Sponsorship, and a Strong Relationship with managers “I receive the help I need to learn and grow professionally.”
Compensation and Promotion Opportunities “The career opportunities here are attractive to me.”
Job Attributes “I get the chance to use my skills and abilities in my job.”
Company Objectives and Aspirations “I understand the company values.”

It is also noted that men don’t accurately assess women’s satisfaction, so if a board of male directors are making decisions on how to improve engagement, they may not have the best judgement. From this, WorkBC follows BCG’s suggestion on listening to senior women. Every organization is struggling due to different problems, so implementing the same solution would not solve each organization’s problems. Each organization needs to consider what are their root causes for disengagement.


There are three common solutions that BCG has provided which will improve not only women’s engagement, but the entire organization’s engagement.


  1. Rewrite the Rules, rethinking communication for feedback, providing flexible work models and reducing gender-based pay gap. Both men and women benefit from stronger feedback where it is grounded and provided actual direction for improvement of both strengths and weaknesses. It also allows for promotion and compensation to be linked directly to performance with justification for any differences between different employees. For employees to perform well in the long run, flexible work models should be available to not only new mothers, but to everyone. This would allow the employees to support their family in times of need.

  2. Support Peer-to-Peer Connections, with connections among peers and shared goals, everyone would benefit more from another person’s success. The focus is on teamwork and providing great support to their peers. A suggested implementation is reducing the weight to failures if employees sought help to the right people. WorkBC supports the promotion of individuals who demonstrate teamwork rather than individual heroes.

  3. Hold Leaders Accountable for Results, allowing leaders to understand organization values and implement them not only in dealing with clients but also employees. Leaders should have the flexibility to use their unique skills to better the organization. This also includes having the KPI metrics for not only employees themselves, but also being responsible for a set amount of promotions and compensation rewards.

Sarah may only be an imaginary character, but she represents the thousands of women who are entering the workforce for the first time. She also represents the girls who were inspired by technology but were deheartened by an endless list of hardships. For Sarah to achieve her career and personal life aspirations, every step from education to hiring to career development are necessary. No matter how talented she may be, she still needs to be given these opportunities to learn and shine in the organization. Only this way, could she become a longterm asset of her organization.
