Three Reasons Why Gender Diversity is Valued by Companies

  • Introduction
March 18, 2020

One of the most prevalent problems we face in the technology industry is the lack of gender diversity. There has been a historical underrepresentation of women across most technology companies in the world, but why is this now being treated as a significant problem by technology giants like Google, Amazon, and Microsoft? Why is gender diversity beneficial to a company, or the lack of gender diversity considered a loss?


There are both quantitative and qualitative evidence which suggest that gender diversity is imperative to a modern company’s success. We will use three facets to define success: financial performance, company reputation, and talent pool.


Diversity leads to better financial performance

Per this report by McKinsey, companies with better diversity tend to have better financial performance overall. Specifically, companies in the top quartile for gender diversity are 15 percent more likely to have financial returns above their national industry medians. For example, in the United Kingdom, for every 10 percent increase in gender diversity, earnings before interest and taxes rose by 3.5 percent.


In a Financial Times article, it was cited that companies with more women on their boards have delivered a 46 percent better return on equity since 2010, in comparison to groups which lack this gender diversity. Thus, in addition in increasing overall gender diversity within a company, it would also be beneficial to increase women representation on senior-executive teams.


Diversity enhances company reputation

In a 2018 survey by Atlassian, 77 percent of respondents think diversity and inclusion are important to their company, 85 percent think that diversity and inclusion are important to employees are their company. From this, we can see the implications that gender diversity may have on a company’s image to both the public and their own employees.


People care about diversity, and think it’s important. With the current buzz on gender diversity, companies performing better in this aspect are portrayed as more progressive and innovative than companies who are doing worse.


Diversity hiring opens untapped talent pools

The notion of company reputation or public image leads us into our next issue of skill shortages. Around 40 percent of companies are facing skill shortages, as competitors draw on the same talent pool as everyone else. A simple solution would be to recruit from non-traditional sources, such as minority groups and associations in tech, for example, local Women in Tech groups.


The untapped labour markets will give a company access to talent with more diverse perspectives, and a diverse group of workers is more likely to do a better job than a homogeneous group. A positive side effect of a more diverse workforce is better engagement, which boosts a company’s private and public image.
